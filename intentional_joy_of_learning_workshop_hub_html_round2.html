Passion

Model authentic excitement. Connect the concept to your story or practice.

Storytelling Demonstrations Personal relevance

Passion → Course Bridge (Sprint)

Time: 8–12 min · Mode: individual → pair share
Steps
  1. List three things you’re passionate about. (Anything—research, music, running, making…)
  2. List three courses you teach.
  3. Connect the dots: For each course, write one sentence on how you already bring a passion into the classroom.
  4. Sprinkle plan (choose 3 micro‑injections): a 60‑sec story, an image/prop, a 30‑sec video, a live mini‑demo, or a personal anecdote that reveals why the concept matters to you.

Writing‑to‑Learn Audit (Keep the WHY)

Time: 10–15 min · Mode: individual → pair consult
Questions
  1. In your assignments, do you have a question where students are plugging numbers into equations? Or do you have a question where they are deriving equations?
  2. Can you convert the question into a writing‑to‑learn approach where students think about the WHY behind the question, perform sanity checks, and provide narrative explanations?
  3. What obstacles do you see in integrating these? (Grading, time, TA training?)

Your responses

AI helper (optional) 
"Act like an expert curriculum designer and learning scientist who specializes in writing-to-learn strategies across disciplines. Your task is to transform a user-submitted problem into a writing-to-learn task — an activity that encourages students to articulate their thinking, deepen understanding, and reflect on content through short written responses.

Input: A content-based problem provided by the user: [paste original problem here].

Objective: Convert the original problem into a writing-to-learn task by generating the following components:

1. A 1-line rationale ("WHY") that explains how writing about this topic will help the student understand the underlying concept more deeply.
2. Three "sanity check" questions students can use to review their thinking or written response for clarity, correctness, or depth (e.g., “Did I use a specific example?”, “Have I explained my reasoning clearly?”, etc.).
3. A writing prompt that asks students to respond in 120–150 words. This prompt should:
   - Encourage reasoning, reflection, or explanation (not recall or regurgitation).
   - Connect explicitly to the concept or problem.
   - Be written in student-friendly language with a clear writing goal.

Creative Control

Let students choose the medium; you assess accuracy and clarity.

MetaphorComicAudio scriptGame mechanic

Creative Control Audit

Time: 12–15 min · Mode: individual
Questions
  1. Out of all your assignments, where can students be “let loose”?
  2. Think of a problem/assignment/project you’re delivering right now where you can integrate creative control.
  3. How can you prompt students to create something you haven’t specifically asked for so they go above and beyond?

Your responses

AI helper (optional) 
"Act like an expert in instructional scaffolding and creative assessment design, with a focus on integrating student voice and agency into technical problem-solving tasks. Your job is to write a paste-ready CREATIVE CONTROL CLAUSE that gives students structured flexibility in how they present or communicate their solution to a specific academic problem.

Inputs:
- Course: [insert course name]
- Learning Goal: [insert specific learning goal]
- Problem:
```[paste problem here]```
- Extra Time Allowed: [insert amount, e.g., “up to 20 minutes”]

Return the following in clean Markdown format:

1. **Creative Control Clause (1 sentence)**: Invite students to choose their preferred media format (e.g., diagram, narrated slide, written paragraph, annotated photo) to explain their solution, while adhering to three strict guardrails:
   - **Accuracy**: The core math/reasoning must be correct and complete.
   - **Clarity**: The communication must be easy to follow for peers.
   - **Transparency**: All key assumptions must be named or labeled.

2. **Beyond-Brief Add-On (1 sentence)**: Suggest one optional extension idea that prompts deeper thinking or transfer — e.g., "Suggest one scenario where your solution would break down and explain why" — without adding grading burden.

3. **Rubric (3 lines)**: Present a simple scoring guide with weights:
   - Accuracy (50 points): Correct logic, steps, and final answer.
   - Clarity (30 points): Format is well-structured and easy to understand.
   - Insight (20 points): Includes thoughtful nuance, edge case, or reflection.

Ensure all elements are tightly aligned with the problem and appropriate for the course and audience level. Keep tone professional but student-friendly.
"
